## Problem Statement

Kraft has no shared, instrumentable answer to "is FOSSE working?" Today the project has no telemetry. The open RFC on the FOSSE P2 ([How will we know if FOSSE is working?](https://fossep2.wordpress.com/2026/04/27/how-will-we-know-if-fosse-is-working/)) framed the problem around *what could we measure* — adoption, activation, activity — but skipped the prior question: what user outcome are we actually trying to detect, in non-metric terms?

Without that anchoring, every metric is a proxy for an unstated goal, and we end up measuring what's easy instead of what matters.

## Success Scene (the user's words)

A blogger or company owner or government employee wants to say something. They go to their site and post it, and it just works. Their site, newsletter, RSS feed all get it (already true via WP+Jetpack). What's new with FOSSE: their Mastodon and Bluesky followers see the post natively — not as an obvious cross-post, but as if the author posted directly to that network. People can like or reply, and the original author gets those reactions back. It feels like a conversation. The site itself displays those reactions, so the home page looks active and engaged. Their home on the web is "homey" — it doesn't feel ignored.

## Leading-indicator scene

Pressed on what observable, if it's good, makes Kraft willing to call FOSSE "working" — and if it's bad, makes him willing to call it "failed":

> "If regular people start using it. If it's only the nerds, it failed. If a regular person could go from start to finish and it just works without them feeling dumb or frustrated."

Operationally defined: WordPress.com Simple plan users. People who don't install plugins, don't want anything fancy, just need a basic website AND can use FOSSE to make it social.

## Premises (agreed in this session)

1. **Q1 (success scene) and Q2 (leading indicator) measure different things, and reconcile cleanly.** Q2 is the *necessary precondition* — non-nerd adoption is what makes scale possible. Q1 is the *sufficient validation* — the round-trip conversation is what proves the project worked. Measure-the-precondition-first, then-measure-the-validation. **— agreed.**

2. **WordPress.com Simple is the leading-indicator population.** Self-host users are nerds (their stated audience); the proof FOSSE crossed into "regular people" territory is wp.com Simple usage. **— agreed.**

3. **FOSSE-on-wp.com-Simple is committed for Radical Speed Month**, contingent on the no-new-tables constraint holding. Verified empirically — zero `dbDelta`, zero `CREATE TABLE`, zero `$wpdb->prefix . 'custom_table'` patterns across FOSSE source plus the bundled wordpress-activitypub and wordpress-atmosphere plugins. All storage lives in standard WP tables (`wp_options`, `wp_posts`/`wp_postmeta`, `wp_comments`/`wp_commentmeta`). Constraint holds. **— agreed.**

4. **Integration shape is "default-on for launched/public/search-indexed Simple sites" PLUS "dedicated entry point" (Cohort A + Cohort B).** Search-engine-indexing setting acts as the federation gate — piggybacks on a permission the user already granted ("I want this content publicly discoverable"), inherits twenty years of WP privacy semantics, no new opt-in dialog required. **— agreed.**

5. **Time horizon is layered.** Single-post funnel as the early-signal report; sustained-activity and differential-liveness as the steady-state quarterly read. **— agreed.**

6. **Doc scope is wp.com Simple primary, self-host as a section.** Self-host's metric story is essentially WP.org install count + GitHub release downloads + an optional opt-out aggregate ping. The wp.com integration is where the success criterion actually lives. **— agreed.**

7. **Consumption / reception side is out of scope.** Another team's work. FOSSE measures the publish path and the round-trip back — not the read-experience side of federated content. **— agreed.**

## Cohort Definitions

### Cohort A — "Volume play" (auto-on Simple sites)

Definition: WordPress.com Simple plan sites that are
- Created after FOSSE-on-Simple ships, AND
- Launched (not in Coming Soon / Private state), AND
- Public-search-indexable (the user did not check "Discourage search engines from indexing this site" in Settings → Reading)

Federation auto-active from site launch. User can disable it (deferred — covered in v2).

This is the "regular people" cohort. It's where the leading-indicator funnel runs.

### Cohort B — "Intent play" (dedicated entry point)

Definition: visitors who arrive at a FOSSE-branded landing page (e.g. fosse.wordpress.com) and create a Simple site through that path. Site has FOSSE pre-configured.

Higher selection bias than Cohort A — these are people who explicitly came looking for federation. Useful for measuring intent-driven conversion separately from default-flow conversion. Won't see the larger Cohort A but should convert at higher rates.

### Cohort C — "Comparison" (FOSSE-off Simple sites)

Definition: WordPress.com Simple plan sites that match Cohort A's criteria except FOSSE is not active (either ineligible by search-indexing setting, or eligible but disabled).

Used for differential liveness measurement — does FOSSE-on change site activity vs FOSSE-off?

## Metrics

### Tier 1 — Early-signal funnel (point-in-time, per cohort)

Run on Cohort A and Cohort B separately. Reportable from week 1 of FOSSE-on-Simple shipping. Each step is a wp.com Tracks event:

| Step | Event | What it measures |
|------|-------|------------------|
| 1 | Site created (Simple, eligible for FOSSE) | Cohort entry |
| 2 | FOSSE active on the site | Per-cohort: 100% for A by definition, conversion rate for B |
| 3 | At least one network connection completed (Mastodon handle OR Bluesky DID) | Setup completion |
| 4 | First post published on a FOSSE-active site | Publishing happened |
| 5 | Post federated as a *native* record on the target network (not link-card fallback) | Render-quality success |
| 6 | Post received ≥1 inbound reaction within 14 days | Engagement (the social moment landed) |
| 7 | Author engaged with that reaction (replied / liked / clicked through) | Round-trip closure (the homey signal) |

Reported as a funnel: % conversion from each step to the next. The shape of the dropoff tells us *where* FOSSE is failing for regular people, not just *whether*.

Steps 2→5 are the Q2 leading indicator (regular people complete the flow).
Steps 5→7 are the Q1 validation (the homey round-trip).

### Tier 2 — Sustained-activity (quarterly, per user)

For sites in Cohort A and B that completed the Tier 1 funnel through step 5, measure over rolling 90-day windows:

- Federated posts published per user per period
- % of those posts that received ≥1 inbound reaction
- % of those reactions the author engaged with
- Time-since-last-federated-post per user (decay distribution)

A site that federated one post in week 1 and nothing since is not "homey." A site that federates regularly and gets sustained engagement is. Sustained-activity is the metric that distinguishes those cases.

### Tier 3 — Differential liveness (quarterly, per cohort)

Compare Cohort A (FOSSE-on) to Cohort C (FOSSE-off, otherwise comparable Simple sites):

- Posts published per site per 90-day window — does FOSSE-on correlate with more publishing?
- Comments per post — does FOSSE-on correlate with more reactions surfacing on the site?
- Site sessions / pageviews per period — does the round-trip drive return visits?

This is the strongest causal claim available without a randomized trial. Requires a clean baseline reading on Cohort C *before* FOSSE-on rolls out broadly, so the comparison isn't contaminated by reverse-causation (sites that opted out of search-indexing being inherently different).

### Self-host signal (long-tail)

The plugin we just shipped is a reference implementation and an evangelism surface, not the volume play. Its metric story:

- WP.org active-install count (public, free, noisy but directional)
- GitHub release download stats (public, free, even noisier)
- Optional: opt-out aggregate pingback for "completed wizard yes/no" + version + active-network presence. Aggregate counts only — no per-user content, no DIDs, no handles. Privacy framing minimal because everything is aggregate; opt-out (not opt-in) because the data is already low-stakes.

This section confirms the open-source side isn't dead, but doesn't drive the success-or-failure call on FOSSE the project.

## Recommended Approach

Ship the metrics in order of effort/value:

1. **Pre-launch (before FOSSE-on-Simple ships):** Establish baseline reading of Cohort C activity. Without this, Tier 3 differential liveness is unmeasurable later.
2. **Week 1 of launch:** Tier 1 funnel events instrumented. Public dashboard internally.
3. **Month 1+:** Tier 2 sustained-activity cohorts begin reporting once enough cohort time has passed.
4. **Quarter 1+:** Tier 3 differential liveness comparison goes live. This is the headline number for "did FOSSE work?"

The self-host signal piggybacks on what's free (WP.org, GitHub) from day 1; the optional pingback is deferred until there's a reason to want more.

## Alternatives Considered

- **Single-cohort design (Cohort A only).** Rejected — losing Cohort B means losing the intent-segmented signal. Adding it costs almost nothing.
- **Point-in-time funnel only as the headline metric.** Rejected — declares success too generously (a single-post miracle isn't homey). Funnel is included as Tier 1, but the headline is differential liveness.
- **Full opt-in pingback for self-host as the primary metric.** Rejected — opt-in pingback from self-host is the hard problem the RFC was wrestling with, and self-host isn't where the leading indicator lives. Solving it isn't a prerequisite for shipping success metrics.
- **No comparison cohort.** Rejected — without Cohort C, "FOSSE-on sites publish more" could just mean "people who would have published more anyway turned FOSSE on." Differential reading is what makes the causal claim defensible.
- **Toggle-in-Settings integration shape (Jetpack Social–style).** Rejected as the primary integration — it self-selects for users who already wanted federation, which collapses the "regular people" cohort to "already-motivated regular people." Default-on for the eligible cohort is the more honest test of whether FOSSE crosses the nerd boundary.

## Open Questions

- **Tracks event schema.** The Tier 1 event names above are placeholders. The wp.com data team will need to confirm names, properties, and which existing events to extend vs add new. Owner: kraft to coordinate with wp.com data team.
- **Cohort C baseline window.** How many weeks of Cohort C activity do we need before FOSSE-on rolls out, to establish a stable comparison baseline? Needs a stats-side judgment call, probably 4–8 weeks. If FOSSE-on ships before that window closes, Tier 3 is degraded.
- **"Native render" detection (step 5).** The FOSSE-published post needs to be classified as a native record vs link-card-fallback at the time of publishing. This requires the publish path to emit which strategy ran (long-form teaser-thread, short-form note, link-card fallback). Already present in the projector code; just needs a Tracks event to emit it.
- **Author-engaged-with-reaction (step 7).** Definition needs sharpening. Does "engaged" mean "replied," "clicked through to bsky.app/mastodon," "saw the notification," or something else? Each is instrumentable but they measure different things. Recommend "replied OR clicked through to source" as the v1 definition; iterate after seeing real data.
- **Privacy disclosure for self-host pingback.** Deferred, but if/when we add the optional aggregate ping, the disclosure copy and opt-out mechanism need explicit design. Not blocking the wp.com metrics.
- **Volume target.** No conversation about expected scale. Are we hoping for 100 active Cohort A sites by EOQ? 10,000? 100,000? The order of magnitude affects whether Tier 1 funnel even produces meaningful percentages early on (low-N percentages are noise).

## Distribution Plan

- **Cohort A:** ships as part of the wp.com Simple integration. Wiring already exists for the bundled AP/Atmosphere on wp.com infrastructure; FOSSE adds the projector layer + the auto-on-when-search-indexed gate.
- **Cohort B:** dedicated landing page on a wp.com subdomain. Feeds into the standard new-Simple-site flow with FOSSE pre-configured.
- **Self-host:** plugin distribution via WP.org and GitHub Releases. Tracking is public counts only.

## What I noticed about how you think

- You re-frame fast. I asked for a measurable signal that separates nerds from non-nerds, expecting you to propose a heuristic. You pivoted entirely and gave me a population: "WordPress.com Simple plan users." That's a sharper move — populations are easier to instrument than heuristics, and Simple is already a defined cohort. But it requires the wp.com integration to be real, which is a strategic dependency you flagged as committed without me asking. You'd already done that mental step.

- You bias toward existing permissions surfaces over new ones. The search-engine-indexing setting as the federation gate isn't a new privacy mechanism — it's a 20-year-old WP setting being reused for a related semantic. That's the kind of choice that comes from someone who's lived with WP long enough to know which doors users have already walked through. Worth naming as a pattern: when a new feature needs a permission gate, look for an existing setting whose semantics already cover the case before designing a new opt-in.

- You took my position pushing engagement-as-leading-indicator and didn't accept it. You re-rooted in adoption. That's defensible — engagement is downstream of adoption, you can't have the round-trip without anyone in it — but it's also a quiet bet that "more people doing it" is more important than "the people doing it have a great experience." Both matter; you picked one as the leading indicator. Fine, but worth holding lightly and revisiting after the first quarter of data.

- You drew a hard "consumption out of scope, another team's work" line mid-session. That's a focus move, not a refusal — you're not pretending consumption doesn't matter, you're saying it isn't FOSSE's problem to solve. Useful in a project this scope-fluid. Make sure that line is shared with the consumption team so neither side accidentally double-builds or leaves a gap.
